Design for everyone

Xinrui | Mon 07 November 2022

What is Accessibility and what is Universal Design

Accessibility allows users of all abilities to understand, use and enjoy a design. Universal Design, which includes accessibility, is the design and composition of an environment so that it can be accessed, understood and used to the greatest extent possible by all people regardless of their age, size, ability or disability. These two concepts are widely used in architectural design. For architectural design, accessible or age-appropriate design, which has emerged in recent years, is an application of universal design. In fact, there are barriers in the web world as well, such as the headache of stairs in architecture for people with disabilities. I will focus more on accessibility and the application of universal design in digital applications, such as web design.

Why are these important

The key fact mentioned by WHO is over 1 billion people live with some form of disability. Also almost everyone can experience some form of temporary or permanent disability at some point in their lives. The World Wide Web is known for its openness and the concept of the interconnectedness of everything, and the failure to guarantee access to it for minorities can be seen as a form of discrimination. In 2021, there are 2,352 website accessibility lawsuits were filed. These two statistics alone are enough to give us an idea of the importance of design accessibility.

The world of people with disabilities

Designers should understand all their users, including those with disabilities. Feeling the world from their point of view is the only way to truly think from the perspective of people with disabilities.

Ways to improve accessibility

In many countries around the world, digital accessibility is explicitly written into the law:

In the UK, website accessibility is a legal requirement for both public and private sector organizations.

"All UK service providers have a legal obligation to make reasonable adjustments under the Equality Act 2010 or the Disability Discrimination Act 1995 (in Northern Ireland).” Central Digital and Data Office, UK Government, 2018

In the US, the increase in litigation has brought more attention to the importance of accessibility compliance.

In 2018, Section 508 was officially updated to include new requirements that reflect technological developments. It also now includes the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), an international standard for accessibility.

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) prohibits discrimination on the basis of disability in employment, State and local government, public accommodations, commercial facilities, transportation, and telecommunications. It also applies to the United States Congress.

More policies and laws could be found here

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) is the most popular digital accessibility standard and the most frequently cited guide in digital accessibility litigation. WCAG 2 provides the following four principles for web design:

1.Perceivable - Information and user interface components must be presentable to users in ways they can perceive.

  Always provides alternatives for content. For example, provide text alternatives for any non-text content.

2.Operable - User interface components and navigation must be operable.

  Make all functionality avalibable from a keyboard and also give user enough time to operate.

3.Readable - Make text content readable and understandable.

  Make sure users could understand text content and could use the website in a predictable way.

4.Compatible - Maximize compatibility with current and future user agents, including assistive technologies.

Conclusion

As laws and policies continue to improve, there is a growing concern about accessibility. We need to be clear that a product designed for the majority only is unfair and discriminatory. As designers, we should focus on the interests of minority groups and experience their lives through simulation. For example, when designing a website, we can use a color blindness simulator to simulate the display of a web page. A simple way to do this is to convert a web page into a grayscale image and see if the text is legible. We can also use a screen reader to determine if a web page is visually impaired friendly. Making a design accessible is actually less difficult than one might think, as long as one realistically considers the difficulties of minority groups.
